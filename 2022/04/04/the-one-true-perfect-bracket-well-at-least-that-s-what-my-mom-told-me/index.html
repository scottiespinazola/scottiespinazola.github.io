2022-04-04

So the time has finally come. I am excited and nervous.

I feel like I am playing in the tournament but that is far from the truth. I played organized basketball for about three years, I think I scored a handful of baskets. I played before my growth spurt so I think that has a lot to do why I am not playing in college right now and preparing for a long NBA career. I am confident that is the soul reason.

When I played I remember also taking care of the ball as that is the most important part of trying to score. Can not score if you don’t have the ball. As well as you can not shot free throws if you don’t have the ball. Those are just poetic ways to say that I will be leading the metrics with rolling turnover percentage as well as rolling free throw percentage coupled with a metric that ranks the schools for how many times they get to the line.

Taking turnovers, an offense can not survive on limited possession and repeatibly shooting themselves in the foot. This makes the most sense; you have more possessions you score more; you score more, with a little help from defense, you win more games; you win more games you make my metric the best in the class. That is all sound logic and I will not be questioning it. Well maybe a little.

Second most important is the free throws. When you think of the best players in theh NBA you like of Brooklyn Nets forward Kevin Durant, Philadelphia 76ers forward/center Joel Embid coupled with teammate guard James Hardin, Chicago Bulls forward Demar Derozen, Dallas Mavericks guard Luka Doncic, Memphis Grizzlies guard Ja Morant and Bucks center Giannas (no chance I spell his name out). what do all of them have in common. They know how to get to the line. Getting to the line is the first piece of the puzzle. Why? Because the name speaks for themselves. FREE throws. free points. And then once you get to the line converting those FREE points into actual points is the real importance. The team who gets the most free points usually wins.

After those metrics the thought process really started. Trying to figure out what all goes into winning basketball got me watching it more. While watching the games I had to focus so I hope me using this class as an excuse to not do all of my other homework was okay. Just joking. But I have been watching more to understand the story of the stats more.

And in doing so the metrics I thought deeper about was effective field goal percentage, rebounding, offensive rating and defensive efficiency.

I think the first three in that list do not need more explaining other than this. If you shoot effecive shots and they go in the majority of the time you win. If you out-rebound the other team it gives you more second chance points as well as holding the other team to limited second chance points. You win the game.

Eficiencies are where it becomes just a very simple case. When any time playing any sport defense tends to way more when it comes to the playoffs and big games. Offenses can function but a defense getting a spot will, for the most part, give the whole story. Defensive efficiency will give a good basis of what defenses are the best in the country and how they have stacked up the whole season. But like we just said. Offenses have to score. And if I have taken anything from the eight weeks that I have been in management class it is that efficiency weighs more than effort. offensive efficiency should be in everyones metric and it will be in mine. teamgames <- load_mbb_team_box(seasons = 2015:2022) %>%
  separate(field_goals_made_field_goals_attempted, into = c("field_goals_made","field_goals_attempted")) %>%
  separate(three_point_field_goals_made_three_point_field_goals_attempted, into = c("three_point_field_goals_made","three_point_field_goals_attempted")) %>%
  separate(free_throws_made_free_throws_attempted, into = c("free_throws_made","free_throws_attempted")) %>%
  mutate_at(12:34, as.numeric)
teamstats <- teamgames %>% 
  group_by(team_short_display_name, season) %>%
  arrange(game_date) %>%
  mutate(
    team_score = ((field_goals_made-three_point_field_goals_made) * 2) + (three_point_field_goals_made*3) + free_throws_made,
    possessions = field_goals_attempted - offensive_rebounds + turnovers + (.475 * free_throws_attempted),
    offensive_efficiency = (team_score/possessions)*100,
    true_shooting_percentage = (team_score / (2*(field_goals_attempted + (.44 * free_throws_attempted)))) * 100,
    turnover_pct = turnovers/(field_goals_attempted + 0.44 * free_throws_attempted + turnovers),
    free_throw_factor = free_throws_made/field_goals_attempted,
    team_rolling_true_shooting_percentage = rollmean(true_shooting_percentage, k=10, align="right", fill=NA),
    team_rolling_turnover_percentage = rollmean(turnover_pct, k=10, align="right", fill=NA),
    team_rolling_free_throw_factor = rollmean(free_throw_factor, k=10, align="right", fill=NA),
    team_rolling_off_ef = rollmean(offensive_efficiency, k=10, align="right", fill=NA)
  ) %>% ungroup()

teamstats <- teamstats %>% 
  select(game_id, team_id, offensive_efficiency) %>%
  mutate(team_id = as.numeric(team_id)) %>% 
  rename(opponent_id = team_id, opponent_offensive_efficiency=offensive_efficiency) %>% 
  left_join(teamstats) %>%
  group_by(team_short_display_name, season) %>%
  arrange(game_date) %>%
  mutate(
    team_rolling_d_eff = rollmean(lag(opponent_offensive_efficiency, n=1), k=10, align="right", fill=NA)
    ) %>% ungroup()

I will hand it to them that they did at-least give Arkansas a run IN THE FIRST ROUND.

Obviously that was terrible. But why?

Where I went wrong was that I tried to be too balanced. I made sure that every metric that I used had another metric to cover for the margin of error. that just doesn’t work like that in basketball and especially in NCAA.

I think the metrics that ended up meaning something were offensive efficiency, defensive efficiency and free throw shooting. when doing my research, sitting and watching every march madness game on my couch at home, it seemed to me that the team that went to the line most, making those free throws, and were the most dominant with scoring on their possessions and stopping the other team when they had the ball was the biggest indicator of who was going to win.

Even though I didn’t get the winner right, along the way my metrics was trying to tell me something with how many upsets I did have. For example I had Baylor losing in the round of 64. Obviously, that didn’t happen but they lost in the round of 32 so ill call that a victory. In that matchup against Norfolk state my metric was 68% sure that Baylor was going to lose. Seems fairly confident coming from a No. 16 seed.

Even though I didn’t call the right round my metric did pick up on Kentucky being frauds. I don’t really know how St. Peters did what they did but it was special. My metric had Kentucky being taken down by Murray State in the round of 32 with 63% of confidence running through it. Even though that was not the matchup or the round that they lost in, one round difference for both of the biggest upsets in the tourney, ill take it.

What I would do differently for next time is what I said previously. I need to realize that not everything in this world should and is balanced. Maybe Thanos was right? Not the point sorry.

But I think that possessions count way more than we realize in these games. Being able to score consistently percentage wise from games that were not in the tournament don’t really mean much when in NCAA the difference between the teams can be so different. Offensive and Defensive efficiency are what I will rock with going forward because those to me showed a lot better outcomes to who would win the game.

And lastly before I go cry because I bet all my money on Vermont, free throws. Seeing that these players are in college and have emotions, these tournament games get to them. Being able to get to the line, first and foremost and then convert the free throws is the most important to me. Villanova this year was top in both of those categories and went to the final four. Does that meaan something? I think yes.

At the end of the day to bank my metrics on 72% confidence wasn’t that smart of an idea but hey, you live and you learn.
